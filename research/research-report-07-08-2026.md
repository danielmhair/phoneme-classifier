# Real-Time On-Device Phoneme Recognition for Children's Speech in Unreal Engine 5: Engineering Research Report

## TL;DR
- **Keep your model choice, change your runtime.** Fine-tuning WavLM-Base+ (or Wav2Vec2) for CTC phoneme recognition is exactly the published state-of-the-art for children's read-aloud phonics (Block Medin, Pellegrini & Gelin, Interspeech 2024, reached 26.1% PER on French 5–8-year-olds by unfreezing transformer blocks). But ship it through **sherpa-onnx's C API (or ONNX Runtime C++ linked directly), not UE5's Neural Network Engine (NNE)** — NNE is still Beta in UE 5.7, historically float-only I/O, lags upstream ORT, and offers no streaming/VAD/feature pipeline; shipping UE speech projects (e.g. MediaPipe4U) already bypass it via sherpa-onnx.
- **Reframe from open phoneme recognition to forced-alignment + Goodness-of-Pronunciation (GOP).** Because a phonics/reading game knows the target word/text, forced alignment (Montreal Forced Aligner or a CTC self-alignment GOP) is far more robust on noisy child speech than free phoneme decoding and directly yields per-phoneme correctness scores for feedback.
- **Buy is largely closed off; build is the right call.** SoapBox Labs — the one purpose-built children's engine with offline/embedded phoneme output — was acquired by Curriculum Associates and is no longer signing new external customers. Remaining commercial options (Speechace, Azure Pronunciation Assessment) are cloud-only, violating your no-cloud/COPPA-friendly requirement. Fine-tuning an open WavLM/Wav2Vec2 phoneme model on children's data (MyST + SpeechOcean762) and running it locally is the best path.

## Key Findings

**1. The commercial "buy" market for on-device children's phoneme recognition has effectively collapsed for new entrants.**
- **SoapBox Labs** was the ideal fit — built from the ground up for children ages 2+, offering both online and offline/embedded deep-learning solutions with real-time phoneme-level output, COPPA/GDPR compliant, integrated into toys/gaming/AR-VR/education. It was **acquired by Curriculum Associates (announced Nov 29, 2023, terms undisclosed)**. Critically, Curriculum Associates said it "will complete existing contracts with SoapBox customers, but does not plan to enter into new contracts with outside organizations." SoapBox now operates as an internal R&D hub powering i-Ready. **It is not available to license for your game.** (For a sense of the accuracy bar it set: SoapBox's own tech blog reports that a six-month Amplify test found its automatic assessment "correlated with human scoring at 96%, a correlation comparable to that of human-to-human scoring.")
- **Speechace** offers real-time phoneme- and syllable-level pronunciation scoring (the JSON returns `phone_score_list` with per-phoneme `quality_score` and "sound_most_like"), and is education-focused — but it is a **cloud HTTP API**, no on-device SDK, commercial key required via sales contact.
- **Azure Pronunciation Assessment** returns phoneme-level accuracy scores (IPA or SAPI), supports streaming mode, and is explicitly marketed for children learning English; Microsoft says it trained on 100,000+ hours across accents/regions/ages including kids. But it is **cloud-only** and priced as standard speech-to-text. Not viable for a no-cloud desktop game.
- **Amira Learning** (Carnegie Mellon lineage, now Pearson-distributed) is a full reading-tutor product, not an SDK; it is cloud/FERPA-based and sold via district licensing (Amira's own NCII pricing and its CEO cite roughly **$20 per student per year** for the full suite — an earlier third-party report of ~$176/child appears to have conflated multi-year or bundled district costs). Not embeddable regardless.
- Newer entrants (Readability, etc.) are consumer apps, not SDKs.

**2. Existing UE plugins do speech-to-text or visemes, not accurate phoneme recognition.**
- **Runtime Speech Recognizer** (Georgy Dev) wraps whisper.cpp for offline cross-platform STT (UE 4.27–5.8, on Fab), with Silero VAD integration and Vulkan GPU acceleration on Windows. It outputs **words, not phonemes**, and Whisper's word-level output is the wrong granularity for phonics feedback. Useful as a reference architecture for audio capture/VAD, not as your recognizer.
- OVRLipSync / MetaHuman audio-to-face produce **visemes for animation**, not phonetically accurate phoneme labels suitable for pronunciation assessment. Do not repurpose them for scoring.

**3. Open-source phoneme models are mature and directly usable.**
- **facebook/wav2vec2-lv-60-espeak-cv-ft** (1.26 GB, Apache-2.0) is a Wav2Vec2-Large fine-tuned on Common Voice to emit eSpeak IPA phonemes via CTC — a strong zero-shot starting point but adult-trained and large.
- **WavLM-Base+** (~94M params) is the best-performing backbone for exactly your use case per Block Medin et al. (Interspeech 2024, Lalilo/Renaissance Learning + IRIT, arXiv:2503.04710): comparing wav2vec 2.0, HuBERT and WavLM for phoneme recognition on 5–8-year-old readers, WavLM-Base+ won, and **"Unfreezing the Transformer block of WavLM base+ gives a PER of 26.1%, a relative reduction of 33.4% compared with the frozen model,"** beating a Transformer+CTC baseline, with better noise robustness across reading tasks. This directly validates the user's WavLM-Base+ instinct.
- **Universal phoneme recognizers** (Allosaurus; Charsiu; wav2vec2-xlsr-53-espeak; ZIPA) exist for IPA output but are adult/multilingual-trained.
- **Newer efficient models:** **Moonshine** (MIT-licensed English, encoder-decoder + RoPE) targets real-time edge ASR — Tiny (~27M params) ~12% WER at 34 ms on a MacBook / 237 ms on Raspberry Pi 5; Small streaming (123M) 7.84% WER at 73 ms — but it is word/token STT, not phoneme CTC. **sherpa-onnx** (k2-fsa) provides streaming Zipformer CTC/transducer models with INT8 quantization, a C API, and a complete pipeline (VAD, features, endpointing); its small English streaming model (~20M) targets Cortex-A7-class CPUs.

**4. Children's speech degrades adult models measurably, and datasets exist to fix it.**
- **Quantified gap (Dutch reading-miscue study, 2024):** at the phoneme level, PER was consistently ~2–4× worse on children than adults — HuBERT-Large 23.1% (child) vs 6.3% (adult); Wav2Vec2-Base 32.6% (child) vs 20.3% (adult); XLSR-Large 36.1% (child). Causes: higher pitch/formants, unstable articulation, developmental phonological errors, spectral variability (per Block Medin et al. and the BabAR paper).
- **Very young children remain hard:** the BabAR study (Lavechin, Bergelson & Levy, 2026; IEEE TASLP + Interspeech 2026) notes prior work (Li et al.) achieved ~60% PER on children aged 1–4; BabAR curates TinyVox (500k+ IPA-transcribed vocalizations, ages 6 mo–8 yr, 5 languages) and finds WavLM slightly best among SSL backbones but PER still high (~55% on babbling-heavy data). **Ages 4–6 will be materially harder than 7–8.**
- **Fine-tuning works with modest data:** Korean speech-sound-disorder wav2vec2 XLS-R hit 10% PER on 137 children/73 words; Kid-Whisper cut MyST WER from 13.93%→9.11% (Small) and 13.23%→8.61% (Medium); an ICASSP-2024 classroom study reached 9.2% WER on MyST.
- **Datasets and licensing:**
  - **MyST (My Science Tutor):** Boulder Learning's overview states **393 hours across 1,371 students in grades 3–5 (ages 8–11), 228,874 utterances, 45% (103,082 utterances, ~210 h) transcribed at word level**; LDC/CLARIN describe the fuller collection as ~470 hours. **Free for non-commercial use (CC BY-NC-SA 4.0)** via myst.cemantix.org; **commercial license separately via Boulder Learning**. Word-level transcripts + pronunciation dictionary — phoneme labels must be derived via G2P/forced alignment. The single most important English child corpus.
  - **SpeechOcean762:** 5,000 utterances, 250 non-native speakers **of whom half are children**, with expert **phoneme-level** accuracy scores (0–2). **Free for commercial and non-commercial use** (OpenSLR #101). The canonical pronunciation-assessment/GOP benchmark.
  - **OGI Kids, CSLU Kids, CMU Kids, PF-STAR:** older, LDC-licensed corpora. **Providence** (CHILDES/PhonBank) covers ages 1–4 with phonetic transcription.
- **On-device is proven feasible:** Dutta & Hansen (2025, arXiv:2507.14451) fine-tuned Whisper tiny.en on MyST to **15.9% WER (11.8% filtered) running on a Raspberry Pi 5 at real-time factor 0.23–0.41** — motivated explicitly by COPPA/GDPR privacy that cloud ASR cannot satisfy. A desktop CPU has far more headroom.

**5. UE5's NNE is the weak link in the user's current architecture.**
- NNE remains a **Beta feature in UE 5.7** ("use caution when shipping with it"). NNERuntimeORT stopped being "Experimental" and became default-enabled in **UE 5.5, which bundles ONNX Runtime 1.17.1** (upstream is far ahead).
- NNE historically supports **only float input/output types** ("text is not yet supported"), and developers reported **int64 handling bugs** — a real problem for tokenized ASR decoder graphs. It requires calling **`SetInputTensorShapes()` before inference and again on every shape change**, with Epic warning against frequent calls — so **variable-length streaming audio incurs buffer re-allocation overhead** and you'll want fixed windows/padding.
- Opset/ORT-version mismatches cause model-load failures on older UE (a dev's opset-16 transformer failed on UE 5.3, worked on 5.5). No developer has published **any** transformer-ASR latency number through NNE — it's untested territory for this workload.
- **What shipping UE speech projects actually do:** integrate **sherpa-onnx via its C API** (the C++ API's move-only types clash with UE's garbage collector and cause allocator-ownership crashes; the C API avoids this). The commercial **MediaPipe4U** plugin embeds sherpa-onnx for offline ASR; Georgy Dev's TTS plugin links ONNX Runtime directly. NNE gives you only raw tensor inference; sherpa-onnx/ORT give you the whole speech pipeline plus version control.
- **Audio capture:** UE's `UAudioCaptureComponent` + submix can record mic input, but you must **resample to 16 kHz mono** (the Audio Mixer Sample Rate is a global setting, so do resampling in your own buffer rather than degrading all game audio), feed a ring buffer, and gate inference with **Silero VAD (ONNX)**.

## Details

### Build vs. Buy verdict
Buy is not viable given your constraints: the only children-specialized engine with offline phoneme output (SoapBox) is off the market, and every other commercial option (Speechace, Azure, ELSA-class) is cloud-based, which conflicts with your local/no-cloud requirement and complicates COPPA compliance for under-13 voice data. **Build.** The open-source ecosystem now provides both the model recipe (WavLM/Wav2Vec2 CTC phoneme fine-tuning) and the runtime (sherpa-onnx/ONNX Runtime) to do this entirely on-device.

### Open phoneme recognition vs. forced alignment (critical design choice)
A phonics/reading game almost always knows what the child is *supposed* to say (the target grapheme, word, or sentence). This turns the problem from hard open-vocabulary phoneme decoding into **forced alignment + pronunciation scoring**, which is dramatically more robust on noisy, disfluent child speech:
- **Montreal Forced Aligner (MFA)** aligns known text to audio at phone and word level; MFA 3.0 (2026) reports mean boundary errors below 15 ms and supports model adaptation. It is heavier (Kaldi/GMM-HMM) and better as an *offline training-data phonemizer* than a real-time runtime.
- **Goodness of Pronunciation (GOP)** is the classic per-phoneme quality metric. Modern **CTC-based, segmentation-free GOP (GOP-SA/GOP-SF, 2025)** works directly on wav2vec2/WavLM CTC posteriors — no pre-segmentation needed — and is the natural scoring layer on top of your fine-tuned model. This gives you exactly the per-phoneme correct/incorrect signal a phonics game needs for feedback.

Use **open phoneme recognition** only for free-play/babble modes where target text is unknown; use **alignment+GOP** for structured reading exercises.

### Model, size, and latency reality check
- **WavLM-Base+ ≈ 94M parameters.** As FP32 that's ~360 MB; **INT8-quantized ONNX ~95–100 MB**, which sherpa-onnx/ORT support. For a short utterance (a single word or short phrase, ~0.5–2 s), utterance-based CTC inference on a modern desktop CPU is comfortably **sub-second, and ~100–300 ms is realistic with INT8 + few threads**. The user's ~100 ms CPU target is achievable for short single-word phonics prompts but **optimistic for full sentences**; budget up to ~1 s for longer utterances or use chunked streaming.
- Prefer **utterance-based inference triggered by VAD end-of-speech** over frame-by-frame streaming for a reading game — it's simpler, more accurate, and the latency (fire once the child stops speaking) is imperceptible in a turn-based phonics loop.
- If you later need true streaming, a **sherpa-onnx streaming Zipformer-CTC** retrained on child speech is the path, but that's a bigger training lift than fine-tuning WavLM.

### Accuracy expectations for educational feedback
- For ages **7–8** (and MyST's grades 3–5), fine-tuned WavLM/Wav2Vec2 phoneme models reach ~20–26% PER, and word-level child ASR reaches <10% WER — **good enough for pronunciation feedback if you (a) score with GOP thresholds tuned per phoneme, (b) require confirmation before marking a child "wrong," and (c) bias toward encouragement.** Azure's own guidance notes children's grading should be less strict than adults'; SoapBox's ~96% human-correlation figure is a realistic ceiling target.
- For ages **4–6**, expect materially worse accuracy; design the game so young-learner modes rely more on alignment/coarse phonetic-category matching (substitutions mostly stay within broad categories, per BabAR) than fine phoneme distinctions.

### Web prototype note
Transformers.js/ONNX.js can run a quantized WavLM/Wav2Vec2 CTC model in-browser via WASM/WebGPU, so your web-prototype path is technically sound for demos. But WASM CPU inference of a 94M-param transformer will be several times slower than native; treat the web build as a prototype/marketing surface, not the accuracy/latency reference.

## Recommendations

**Recommended architecture (hybrid build):** Fine-tuned **WavLM-Base+ CTC phoneme model → INT8 ONNX → sherpa-onnx C API (or ORT C++) inside UE5**, with **Silero VAD gating** and **CTC-GOP scoring against known target text**. Desktop-first, fully local.

**Phase 0 — De-risk the runtime (1–2 weeks).** Before more model work, prove the *integration*: take the existing ONNX WavLM/Wav2Vec2 phoneme model and run it via **sherpa-onnx's C API in a UE5 plugin** (not NNE). Wire `UAudioCaptureComponent` → 16 kHz mono ring buffer → Silero VAD → inference. Benchmark real latency on your min-spec CPU. *Benchmark that changes the plan:* if end-to-end latency for a single-word utterance exceeds ~500 ms, switch to INT8 and/or a distilled/smaller backbone.

**Phase 1 — MVP with off-the-shelf phonemes (2–4 weeks).** Ship an internal build using `wav2vec2-lv-60-espeak-cv-ft` (or the existing prototype model) + CTC-GOP against target text. Validate the *game loop and feedback UX* on adult and older-child test audio. Accept that young-child accuracy is poor here — this phase is about plumbing and pedagogy, not final accuracy.

**Phase 2 — Fine-tune on children's data (4–8 weeks).** Fine-tune WavLM-Base+ with **transformer blocks unfrozen** (the Block Medin et al. recipe) on **MyST** (phonemized via MFA/G2P) plus **SpeechOcean762** (already phoneme-scored, commercially licensed). Apply **child-oriented augmentation: pitch perturbation, VTLP/vocal-tract-length perturbation, speed perturbation, and classroom-babble noise** (all shown to help child ASR). Target ≤25% PER on a held-out child set. *Threshold:* if PER on your youngest cohort (4–6) stays above ~40%, restrict those ages to alignment/coarse-category feedback rather than phoneme-precise correction.

**Phase 3 — Optimize & ship (2–4 weeks).** INT8-quantize, verify operator coverage in ORT, lock model size (~100 MB), set per-phoneme GOP thresholds calibrated to human labels (aim for high precision on "correct," conservative on "wrong"), and localize thresholds by age band.

**Licensing actions:** MyST is free for non-commercial R&D but **you must obtain a commercial license from Boulder Learning before shipping**; SpeechOcean762 is free for commercial use; verify LDC terms if you add OGI/CSLU/CMU Kids. Fine-tuned WavLM (MIT) and Wav2Vec2 (Apache-2.0/MIT depending on checkpoint) backbones are commercially usable — confirm the specific checkpoint license.

**On the user's existing WavLM+ONNX+NNE plan:** **Keep the model and the ONNX export; replace NNE with sherpa-onnx/ORT; add VAD + forced-alignment/GOP.** The model choice is validated by the literature; NNE is the risk (Beta, float-only history, int64 bugs, ORT lag, no speech pipeline, zero published transformer-ASR latency data). This is the single highest-value change.

## Caveats
- **Latency figures are estimates.** No one has published transformer-phoneme-model latency through UE5 NNE, and few through sherpa-onnx-in-UE; the ~100 ms–1 s range is inferred from Moonshine/Whisper CPU RTF data and WavLM parameter count, and must be measured on your target hardware (Phase 0).
- **INT8 on NNE specifically is unverified**; it is supported by underlying ONNX Runtime, which is another reason to link ORT/sherpa-onnx directly.
- **Young-children (4–6) accuracy is a genuine risk.** Published PER for under-6 remains high; do not promise phoneme-precise correction for the youngest users without your own validation data.
- **MyST/child-corpus transcripts contain noise** (mislabeled files, auto-transcriptions); budget cleaning time, and remember MyST is grades 3–5 — it does **not** cover ages 4–6, so you may need to collect/commission your own young-child data (with COPPA-compliant consent) for that band.
- **Exact bundled ORT version in UE 5.6/5.7 is unconfirmed** (5.5 = 1.17.1 confirmed); if you did stay on NNE, verify the shipped ORT/opset supports your exported ops.
- **Numbers cross-corpus are not directly comparable** (different test sets, languages, phone sets); treat PER/WER figures as directional, not head-to-head.
